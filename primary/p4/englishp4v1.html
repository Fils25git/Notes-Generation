2.6. Comparatives of Adjectives

Comparatives are words used to compare two things, people or personalities .
There are some simple rules to keep in mind when forming comparatives .

1. For most adjectives "er" or "r" is added for the comparative form.

Examples:

  1. Tall → Taller
  2. Short → shorter
  3. Strong → stronger
  4. Kind → kinder
  5. Long → longer
  6. Large → larger
  7. Nice → nicer

2. For adjectives ending with "y" is removed and "ier" is added for comparative form .

Examples:

  1. Easy → easier
  2. Happy → happier
  3. Lazy → lazier
  4. Heavy → heavier
  5. Funny → funnier

3. Adjectives for more than three syllables "more" is added before adjective for comparative form .

Examples:

  1. Intelligent → more intelligent
  2. Beautiful → more beautiful
  3. Difficult → more difficult
  4. Handsome → more handsome
  5. Talkative → more talkative

8.5 Determiners of Quantity

Quantifiers state precisely or suggest approximately the amount or the number of a noun. (They quantify) They can be grouped by the noun types they quantify. These expressions are mainly "some, any, many, much, a lot of, a little, a few …etc."

Usage Examples:

  • "Some" is often used in affirmative statements
  • "Any" is often used in negative sentences and questions
  • "Many and Much" express a large quantity

Examples:

  1. I have many postcards.
  2. She has got much influence on you.
  3. There isn't much sugar in the kitchen.
  4. There aren't many people in the streets

"A few" vs "Few":

"A few" expresses a small quantity. "Few" implies that something is not many, not enough or almost none. It expresses a negative idea.

I have a few close friends in town, and we have a very good time together.
I have few friends in town, so I feel lonely from time to time.

"A little" vs "Little":

"A little" expresses a small quantity. "Little" implies that something is not much, not enough or almost none. It expresses a negative idea.

We have a little milk. Let's make a cake.
We have little milk. We can't make a cake.
