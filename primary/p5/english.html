1.1 Telling Stories

What is a story?

A story is a description of real or imagined events. It has characters, a setting, and a plot (what happens).

Characters:

  • Characters are the people, animals, or things in the story.
  • Example 1: In the story "The Mighty," the characters are a big tree and a small plant.
  • Example 2: In the story "Liz at the Market," the characters are Liz and her mother.
  • Example 3: In "The Lion and the Mouse," the characters are a lion and a mouse.

Story Examples:

Example Story 1: "The Lost Money"
Characters: Liz, her mother
Setting: At home and in the market
Plot: Liz's mother gives her money to go to the market. Liz loses the money on the way. She goes back home and her mother finds the money under the table.

Example Story 2: "The Helpful Friend"
Characters: Tom, his friend Sam
Setting: At school
Plot: Tom forgot his homework. His friend Sam shared his book so Tom could copy the work. The teacher praised both for helping each other.

How a story is told:

Stories can be told through:

  • Speaking (like a parent telling a bedtime story)
  • Reading (from a book)
  • Singing or dance (in songs and traditional performances)
  • Drama (acting it out)

1.2 Recounting Past Events

Past Tense Explained:

We use past tense to talk about actions that are finished. There are two types of verbs:

Type Rule Examples
Regular verbs Add -ed to the base verb like → liked
play → played
watch → watched
help → helped
Irregular verbs Change form completely go → went
eat → ate
see → saw
take → took

Time words for past tense:

Use these words to show when something happened in the past:

yesterday, last night, last week, last month, last year, two days ago, in 2020

Example sentences:

1. Yesterday, I watched television. (regular verb)

2. Last week, my family went to visit our grandparents. (irregular verb)

3. Two days ago, I ate rice and beans for lunch. (irregular verb)

4. In 2020, we moved to a new house. (regular verb)

1.4 Recounting Past Experiences

How to talk about past experiences:

Use the past tense and include these details:

  • What happened (the main event)
  • When it happened (time words)
  • Who was there (people involved)
  • Where it happened (place)
  • How you felt (your emotions)

"Last year, my family visited Lake Kivu. We swam in the lake and ate grilled fish. My father, mother, and sister were there. I felt very happy and excited."

Example 2:

"Two months ago, I helped my grandmother in her garden. We planted vegetables together. It was tiring but I felt proud of our work."

2.1 Following Instructions

What are instructions?

Instructions are step-by-step directions that tell you how to do something.

Key words for instructions:

  • First
  • Next
  • Then
  • After that
  • Finally
  • Before you begin
  • Make sure

Example of following instructions:

1. First, take out your English book.

2. Next, turn to page 25.

3. Then, read the instructions carefully.

4. After that, answer the questions.

5. Finally, check your answers.

Tips for following instructions:

  • Read all instructions before starting
  • Ask if you don't understand something
  • Follow the steps in order
  • Check your work when finished

2.4 Defining

What is defining?

Defining means explaining what something means in simple words.

How to define words:

  1. Say what category it belongs to
  2. Explain its main characteristics
  3. Give an example

Format for definitions:

Examples:

Teacher: A teacher is a person who helps students learn.

2.5 Contrasting

What is contrasting?

Contrasting means showing differences between two things.

Contrast words:

  • but
  • however
  • while
  • whereas
  • on the other hand
  • in contrast
  • different from

Examples of contrasting:

1. Math is difficult, but English is easy for me.

2. I like reading books. However, my friend prefers watching movies.

3. While Science has experiments, History has stories about the past.

4. Pencils write with graphite, whereas pens write with ink.

Contrasting school subjects:

• English uses letters and words, while Math uses numbers and symbols.

• Science studies the natural world, but Social Studies studies people and societies.

• Art is creative and visual, whereas Music is creative with sounds.

2.7 Planning and Evaluating Writing

Planning before writing:

  1. Think about your topic
  2. Brainstorm ideas
  3. Organize your ideas
  4. Make an outline
  5. Write your first draft

   A. It helps me communicate

   B. I enjoy reading stories

Evaluating your writing:

  1. Did I answer the question?
  2. Are my sentences complete?
  3. Is my spelling correct?
  4. Did I use punctuation (. , ? !)?
  5. Does it make sense?

  • Missing capital letters at beginning of sentences
  • Missing periods at end of sentences
  • Spelling errors
  • Incomplete sentences
  • Wrong verb tense

Improving your writing:

  1. Read your work aloud
  2. Ask a friend to read it
  3. Correct mistakes
  4. Rewrite neatly
  5. Show it to your teacher

3.7 Skimming and Scanning

What is skimming?

Skimming means reading quickly to get the main idea.

When to skim:

  • To see if a text is useful
  • To get the general idea quickly
  • To review what you already read

How to skim:

  1. Read the title and headings
  2. Look at pictures and captions
  3. Read the first and last sentences of paragraphs
  4. Notice bold or italic words
  5. Read the introduction and conclusion

What is scanning?

Scanning means looking for specific information quickly.

When to scan:

  • To find a name, date, or number
  • To answer specific questions
  • To locate a particular fact

How to scan:

  1. Know what information you need
  2. Move your eyes quickly over the text
  3. Look for key words related to your search
  4. Stop when you find what you need
  5. Read that section carefully

Skimming vs. Scanning:

Skimming: "What is this text mainly about?"

  7. Final touches: Clean, polish if needed

  • Collect dry grass.

  • Weave the base first.
  • Add colored threads for patterns.
  • Don't pull too hard.
  • Be careful with sharp edges.

For more polite instructions:

  • You should use dry materials.
  • It's important to secure the base well.
  • Make sure the edges are smooth.

10.4 Describing a Country

Elements to Describe a Country:

Element What to Include Example for Rwanda
Location Continent, neighbors, coordinates East Africa, borders 4 countries
Size Area, comparison to others 26,338 km², small but densely populated
Geography Mountains, rivers, lakes, climate Land of thousand hills, temperate climate
Population Number, density, major cities ~13 million, Kigali capital
Languages Official, national, common Kinyarwanda, English, French, Swahili
Economy Main industries, exports Agriculture, tourism, tea/coffee exports
Culture Traditional practices, festivals Umuganura festival, traditional dances
Interesting facts Unique features, achievements Cleanest country in Africa, gorilla tourism

Sample Country Description:

Kenya is a country in East Africa. It borders Tanzania, Uganda, South Sudan, Ethiopia, and Somalia. Kenya has a coastline on the Indian Ocean. The capital city is Nairobi. Kenya is known for its wildlife and national parks like the Maasai Mara. Mount Kenya, the second highest mountain in Africa, is located there. The official languages are English and Swahili. Kenya's economy depends on agriculture, tourism, and services. It is famous for long-distance runners.

Grammar Tip: Present Simple for Country Facts

Use present simple for permanent facts about countries:

  • Rwanda is in East Africa.
  • It has about 13 million people.
  • The capital is Kigali.
  • Rwanda borders four countries.
  • People speak Kinyarwanda and English.
  • The country exports coffee and tea.

Use "there is/are" for existence:

  • There are many hills in Rwanda.
  • There is a lake called Kivu in the west.
  • There are several national parks.

Remember: Add -s for he/she/it: Kenya has, Tanzania borders
